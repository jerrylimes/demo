Working Horses With Jim: A Pause, A Legacy & A Lot Of Heart

Today the entire StreamHorse community sends its love, support, and deep appreciation to Jim and Brenda Gordon, the heartbeat behind Working Horses With Jim, the beautifully shot, deeply authentic, and wildly beloved YouTube channel with more than 115,000 loyal subscribers. Their announcement that they are taking a break from creating new content while Jim recovers from hand surgery comes as a quiet shock across the draft horse world, but anyone who knows Jim understands that healing cannot be rushed. The work he does, and the life he lives, requires hands that are steady, strong, and fully restored.

The roots of that life run deep. Jim and Brenda moved to Moira, New York in 1991 and built the Gordon Family Farm and Sawmill into a diversified, season-driven operation. Today the farm shifts fluidly with the needs of the land and community. Jim saws and sells live edge slabs and hardwood lumber. He harvests logs with the horses and supplies them to local sawmills for dimensional lumber. Together they manage standing timber responsibly and raise a small beef herd with care. Summers bring haying, again with the horses doing the work. Winters, when weather allows, transform the property into a postcard with sleigh rides to a heated cabin beside their ice skating pond, a place where families gather and time seems to soften around the edges.

The YouTube channel grew out of Jim’s desire to share what he has learned from decades of working with draft horses. His purpose has always been at once humble and ambitious. He wants to honor the older generation who taught him, pass on the techniques and mindset required to work horses well, and help people remember that a life powered by horses is still possible today. He has always believed in pairing the strength and intelligence of horses with the efficiency of modern machinery, blending the best of both worlds. In his videos you see horses logging, plowing, haying, delivering sleighs full of families through fresh snow, even competing from time to time. What stands out most is not the variety of jobs but the seamless way Jim and the horses communicate. You watch long enough and you begin to understand why so many viewers feel that these animals aren’t just partners in labor, but partners in life.
